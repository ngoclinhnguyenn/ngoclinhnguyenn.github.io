Stony Brook Laboratory for Computational Neurodiagnostics

Research Assistant

From the summer of 2016 to January of 2017, I worked with Dr. Lilianne Mujica-Parodi to simulate regulatory dynamics in the fear response circuit in the brain. Using MatLab Simulink, I modeled Dr. Mujica-Parodi's theory that recklessness and anxiety lie on a spectrum of hyper- and hypo-regulation of the fear response. Using the same statistical analysis on this model as with previous medical data, we obtained matching results, providing support for this theory.

Creating and using a computer-simulated regulatory circuit present for fear in the brain, we were able to unify the regulatory dynamics previously seen across the spectrum for threat detection under one theory. Through this control systems approach, we demonstrate the effectiveness of such an approach for studying and diagnosing regulatory systems within the body.

This paper was published in Frontiers Sys. Neuroscience.

Snake

ECE 3140 Embedded Systems

With a partner, we recreated the classic arcade game Snake on a microcontroller. Using the K64F platform with an Arm chip, we programmed the game logic, rendering functions, and the control logic in C and C++. We took advantage of some existing mbed libraries, but only as skeleton to build off from. The snake game starts as a single dot, and grows in size as more food is eaten. The direction the snake moves towards is controlled by the onboard accelerometer on the K64F.

LV Fusebox

Cornell FSAE

As part of the electrical team on FSAE for ARG19 (our 2019 car), I designed and manufactured the Low Voltage fusebox.

The low voltage fusebox's primary functionality is to distribute power to the components of the car while providing switching, fusing, and current monitoring. The primary challenge for this year was converting our old fusebox design from our previous combustion car for our new all-electric system.

As we previously had overheating issues with our previous fusebox due to high on-state resistance using p-channel MOSFETs, I decided to switch to n-channel MOSFETs for lower on-state resistance. With the n-channel MOSFETs, I needed gate drivers to bring MOSFETs into saturation.

For current monitoring, I used a STMF0 microcontroller with Hall-effect sensing chips. However, the current monitoring development was cut short due to other failures in the accumulator design preventing ARG19 from driving.

Check out the schematic and PCB layout!

Neural Network Drone

Personal Project

Using two webcams attached to a Raspberry Pi controlled RC car, I wrote a python program implementing OpenCV, numPy, and PyBrain to train a back-propagating neural network that can detect obstacles and control the vehicle. Using stereoscopic cameras, the difference between each image was used to discern obstacles in front of the vehicle. By training a neural network to transform a left image to a right image, the difference between a transformed right image and an image from the right camera would approximately determine the location of the obstacle.

This project took inspiration from a CMU graduate study using a simple neural network for obstacle detection with a similar stereoscopic camera setup.

Find my github repo for this project here!

This code was pretty messy as I took examples from online sources and spent a lot of time messing around with code to learn about neural networks.

Juul.io

BigRed\\Hacks 2019 Hardware Hack Winner

Recent news headlines have brought to light the growing teenage vaping epidemic. The uncontrolled usage of addictive nicotine e-cigarettes has flocked the country, with companies like Juul focusing advertising on younger audiences. Especially on college campuses, nicotine addiction is becoming ever more prevalent. Juul.io is a solution to curb and reduce nicotine addiction by allowing Juul users to better understand their e-cigarette usage by tracking it in real time. It would also collect and distribute crucial health data (that Juul itself has been reluctant to provide) about a relatively new, niche, and unknown market leading to more accurate treatments and behavior predictions.

With a team of 3, Nicole Lin, Annie Kimmel, and I worked on making an IoT adapter for the popular e-cigarette product Juul. The adapter measures the amount of current drawn from the device and can collect usage data. By providing users with data on their habits, users become more aware of how much nicotine they are ingesting. This provides both a platform for IoT driven plans for quitting nicotine addiction and a way for users to track their usage.

To integrate data collection to the Google Cloud platform, we used an Arduino microcontroller with WiFi to upload statistics to the cloud each time the Juul device was activated.
